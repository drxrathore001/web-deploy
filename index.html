A. Two-Mark Questions (Short Answer)

1. Define Artificial Intelligence (AI).
AI is a system that observes its environment, processes inputs, and takes actions to achieve a specific goal.
2. State any two objectives of Artificial Intelligence.
1) To build systems that exhibit intelligent behavior, learn, and advise humans.
2) To understand and model human cognitive processes computationally.
3. What is meant by an intelligent agent?
An intelligent agent is a system that uses sensors to gather data from its environment and applies decision rules or learning methods to choose the right action to achieve a goal.
4. Define a rational agent.
A rational agent is one that acts to maximize its expected performance measure based on the data it has perceived and its built-in knowledge.
5. List any two foundations of Artificial Intelligence.
Philosophy (logic, reasoning, mind) and Mathematics (probability, algorithms, optimization).
6. What is an agent–environment interaction?
It is the continuous cycle where an agent observes its environment through sensors, processes the data, and acts upon the environment using its actuators.
7. Mention any two applications of Artificial Intelligence.
Healthcare (for diagnosis) and Finance (for fraud detection).
8. What is a performance measure in the context of intelligent agents?
An objective criterion or metric used to evaluate how successful an agent is at achieving its intended goals or desired outcomes.
9. Define environment in Artificial Intelligence.
The external world, domain, or condition set in which an agent operates, perceives inputs, and executes its actions.
10. State any two characteristics of an intelligent agent.
1) They adapt to changing situations in their environment.
2) They evaluate outcomes and adjust behavior over time.
11. What are sensors in an intelligent agent?
Sensors are the mechanisms or inputs an agent uses to gather data and perceive the state of its environment.
12. What are actuators in an intelligent agent?
Actuators are the mechanisms or outputs through which an agent carries out its chosen actions to alter or affect its environment.
13. Mention any two important milestones in the history of AI.
1) The 1956 Dartmouth Conference where the term "Artificial Intelligence" was coined.
2) The development of deep learning models in the 2010s that revolutionized computer vision and natural language processing.
14. What is meant by autonomy of an agent?
Autonomy is an agent's ability to operate independently, learning and adapting its behavior based on its own experience rather than relying strictly on pre-programmed human knowledge.
15. Define learning agent.
A learning agent is an AI that gathers experience through interaction, evaluates feedback, and updates its behavior to perform better with each cycle.
16. What is meant by PEAS? Expand the term.
PEAS is a framework used to define an agent's operational parameters. It stands for Performance measure, Environment, Actuators, and Sensors.
17. What is a task environment?
The task environment is the specific "problem" an agent is designed to solve, formally defined by the PEAS framework.
18. Distinguish between an agent and an environment (any two points).
1) The agent acts and makes decisions, whereas the environment is the space that receives those actions.
2) The agent contains decision rules and memory, while the environment generates the inputs and feedback the agent learns from.
19. What is meant by artificial intelligence as a science?
It is the interdisciplinary scientific study focused on understanding the principles of intelligence and building computational models that replicate cognitive functions like reasoning and problem-solving.
20. List any two components of an intelligent agent.
Sensors (to gather data) and decision rules/learning methods (to choose the right action).

B. Five-Mark Questions (Brief Answer)

1. Explain the concept of Artificial Intelligence with suitable examples.
Artificial Intelligence is the simulation of human intelligence by machines, enabling them to process information, adapt to situations, and make autonomous decisions. Unlike regular programs with fixed instructions, AI evaluates outcomes and adjusts its behavior. For example, a recommendation engine uses AI to analyze your viewing habits and suggest movies, or an autonomous vehicle processes real-time camera data to navigate traffic safely.
2. Describe the foundations of Artificial Intelligence.
AI is built on several core disciplines:
  • Philosophy: Provides theories on logic, reasoning, and the nature of the mind.
  • Mathematics: Supplies the formal rules for logic, probability (to handle uncertainty), and algorithms.
  • Computer Science: Provides the hardware and programming frameworks required to build and execute AI models.
  • Psychology & Neuroscience: Inspires how machines can mimic human cognitive processes and neural networks.
3. Briefly explain the history and evolution of Artificial Intelligence.
AI's history began in the mid-20th century with Alan Turing's theoretical work and the official founding of the field at the 1956 Dartmouth Conference. Early years were marked by high optimism and symbolic logic programming. The field experienced "AI Winters" in the 1970s and 1980s when funding collapsed due to unfulfilled promises. The 1990s brought a shift toward machine learning and statistical methods. In the 2010s, massive datasets and powerful GPUs fueled the deep learning revolution, leading to modern highly capable AI systems.
4. Explain different types of intelligent agents.
There are five primary types of agents:
  • Simple Reflex Agents: React instantly to current inputs using fixed condition-action rules.
  • Model-Based Agents: Maintain an internal state to track unseen aspects of the environment.
  • Goal-Based Agents: Evaluate possible steps and compare choices to achieve long-term goals.
  • Utility-Based Agents: Assign utility scores to outcomes to choose the most beneficial result.
  • Learning Agents: Gather experience, evaluate feedback, and improve performance over time.
5. Describe the structure of an intelligent agent with a neat diagram.
The structure of an intelligent agent is fundamentally divided into its architecture (the computing device) and the agent program (the algorithm). The agent perceives the state of the environment through its sensors. This percept is passed to the agent program, which uses reasoning, internal models, or learning mechanisms to decide on the best action. The chosen action is then executed back onto the environment via actuators.
6. Explain the functions of an intelligent agent.
The core functions of an intelligent agent are:
  • Perception: Using sensors to gather dynamic data from the surroundings.
  • Decision-Making: Applying decision rules, probability algorithms, or utility evaluations to determine the best course of action.
  • Execution: Carrying out the selected action via actuators to change the environment.
  • Learning: Adapting to new data, evaluating past outcomes, and updating behavior to handle evolving patterns.
7. Discuss the role of environment in agent-based systems.
The environment defines the complexity of the agent required. For a predictable, fully observable environment, a fast simple reflex agent is enough. However, if the environment is dynamic, partially unobservable, or noisy, the agent must rely on internal models and memory to track context. The environment's constraints dictate whether an agent needs to plan goals, weigh utility, or actively learn to succeed.
8. Explain PEAS with an example.
PEAS defines the task environment for an AI system. For an Automated Taxi:
  • P (Performance Measure): Reaching the destination safely, minimizing time, following traffic laws, passenger comfort.
  • E (Environment): Roads, other vehicles, pedestrians, traffic lights, weather conditions.
  • A (Actuators): Steering wheel, accelerator, brakes, indicators, horn.
  • S (Sensors): Cameras, Lidar, GPS, speedometer, engine sensors.
9. Describe various applications of Artificial Intelligence in real life.
AI spans numerous industries based on task complexity. In healthcare, AI agents assist with patient diagnosis. In finance, they are utilized for fraud detection and risk assessment. Everyday digital life relies heavily on learning agents for e-commerce recommendation engines and personalized digital assistants. Furthermore, AI powers transportation through autonomous vehicles and navigation optimization.
10. Explain the concept of rationality in intelligent agents.
Rationality is the property of an agent choosing the action that maximizes its expected performance measure, given its current percept sequence and the knowledge it holds. It does not mean the agent is omniscient (knowing the absolute future) or perfect; rather, it means the agent processes available information logically to make the most optimal choice possible at that exact moment.

C. Ten-Mark Questions (Essay Type)

1. Define Artificial Intelligence. Explain its foundations and applications in detail.
Definition: An AI agent is a system that observes its environment, processes inputs, and takes actions to achieve a specific goal. In contrast to regular software programs that follow fixed instructions, AI systems adapt to situations, respond to environmental changes, evaluate outcomes, and adjust their behavior over time.

Foundations: AI is built upon multidisciplinary foundations including Philosophy (logic, reasoning, mind), Mathematics (probability, optimization, algorithms), Computer Science (hardware, programming), and Psychology/Neuroscience (cognitive models, neural networks).

Applications:
  • Healthcare: AI agents are actively used for medical diagnosis and patient data analysis.
  • Finance: Systems utilize AI for high-frequency trading and fraud detection.
  • Transportation: Learning agents form the core of autonomous vehicles, and multi-agent systems manage traffic networks.
  • E-commerce & Personalization: AI drives recommendation engines and personalized digital assistants.
  • Manufacturing: Agents are deployed for automation, adaptive robotics, and coordinating logistics tasks.
2. Trace the history of Artificial Intelligence and discuss its major developments.
The history of AI can be traced back to the mid-20th century. It officially began as a field at the 1956 Dartmouth Conference, organized by John McCarthy, where the term "Artificial Intelligence" was coined.

Major Developments:
  • Early Enthusiasm (1950s-1960s): Development of symbolic logic and early programs like the General Problem Solver (GPS) and ELIZA, an early natural language processing program.
  • AI Winters (1970s & late 1980s): Periods of reduced funding and interest due to computational limitations and the realization that early AI models could not easily scale to real-world complexity.
  • Expert Systems (1980s): The rise of systems programmed with rules from human experts, finding commercial success in specific corporate domains.
  • Machine Learning Era (1990s-2000s): A shift from symbolic logic to statistical approaches, driven by increases in computational power and data.
  • Deep Learning Revolution (2010s-Present): The explosion of neural networks, powered by GPUs and massive datasets, leading to breakthroughs in computer vision, autonomous driving, and large language models.
3. Explain intelligent agents in detail. Discuss their structure and functions.
Functions: The primary function of an AI agent is to observe its environment, process inputs, and take action to achieve a goal. Rather than strictly following fixed instructions, they evaluate outcomes, respond to changes, and adapt their behavior, which makes them highly useful for automation and real-time decision-making. Agents can also operate in multi-agent systems where they must cooperate, compete, share information, or divide responsibilities.

Structure:
  • Sensors: Agents use sensors to gather data from the environment.
  • Decision Mechanisms (Agent Program): They utilize decision rules or learning methods to choose the right action for every step. They may also use internal models, probability estimates, or utility evaluations to handle uncertainty.
  • Memory: Agents can possess memory to track past states, handle partial information, and understand context, which improves performance in dynamic conditions.
  • Actuators: The hardware or software mechanisms used to implement the decision and alter the environment.
4. Discuss various types of intelligent agents with suitable examples.
The foundational types of AI agents vary in memory use, adaptability, and decision-making abilities:
  • Simple Reflex Agents: React exclusively to current inputs following predefined condition-action rules and do not store past information. Example: Thermostats and automatic doors, which operate in fully observable and predictable environments.
  • Model-Based Agents: Maintain an internal state to track unseen portions of their environment, combining current perceptions with stored information. Example: Adaptive control systems and robotics that must consider past states before acting.
  • Goal-Based Agents: Evaluate possible steps and compare choices to select the path that moves them closer to a long-term outcome. Example: Navigation and complex problem-solving systems that require search and planning techniques.
  • Utility-Based Agents: Assign utility scores to potential results and select the action that yields the highest benefit. Example: Systems operating in uncertain environments that must balance multiple valid choices, such as trading off speed versus safety.
  • Learning Agents: Gather experience through interaction, evaluate feedback, and update their behavior to perform better over time. Example: Autonomous vehicles and recommendation engines where patterns evolve and rules cannot be pre-programmed.
5. Explain agent–environment interaction. Classify different types of environments.
Interaction: Agents continuously interact with their environment by using sensors to gather data and applying decision rules to execute actions via actuators. They adapt to the environment by evaluating outcomes and adjusting behavior.

Environment Classification: Environments heavily dictate agent design and can be classified as:
  • Fully Observable vs. Partially Observable: Fully observable environments give the agent access to the complete state of the environment at all times (suitable for simple reflex agents). Partially observable environments (noisy or incomplete data) require model-based agents with memory.
  • Deterministic vs. Stochastic: Deterministic means the next state is completely determined by the current state and the agent's action. Stochastic environments involve uncertainty and randomness.
  • Static vs. Dynamic: Static environments don't change while the agent is deliberating. Dynamic environments have evolving patterns and require fast decision-making or learning capabilities.
  • Discrete vs. Continuous: Discrete environments have distinct, clearly defined states and actions (like chess). Continuous environments change fluidly (like driving a car).
6. Describe applications of Artificial Intelligence in healthcare, education, and industry.
  • Healthcare: AI agents are utilized for accurate medical diagnosis, medical image analysis (like X-rays and MRIs), robotic surgeries, and personalized medicine planning.
  • Industry (Manufacturing & Finance): In manufacturing, AI is used for automation, predictive maintenance of machines, and adaptive robotics. The finance industry uses agents for fraud detection, algorithmic trading, and credit scoring. Logistics relies heavily on multi-agent systems for supply chain optimization.
  • Education: AI is used for personalized learning systems that adapt to a student's pace, automated grading of assignments, intelligent tutoring systems, and identifying students who may need additional academic support.

A. 2 Mark Questions (Very Short Answer)

1. What is Knowledge Representation (KR)?
KR is the method of formulating knowledge about the real world in a structured form so that a computer system can utilize it to solve complex tasks.
2. Define a Knowledge-Based Agent.
A Knowledge-Based Agent is an intelligent agent that maintains an internal state of knowledge (a Knowledge Base) and uses reasoning mechanisms to decide what actions to take.
3. What is a Knowledge Base?
A Knowledge Base (KB) is a centralized repository of facts, rules, and relationships used by an AI system to reason and make decisions.
4. State any two components of a Knowledge-Based System.
1) The Knowledge Base (KB)
2) The Inference Engine.
5. What is declarative knowledge?
Declarative knowledge consists of factual information about the domain (knowing "what" is true), such as facts and rules.
6. Define procedural knowledge.
Procedural knowledge is information on how to perform a task (knowing "how"), often represented as a sequence of steps, rules, or instructions.
7. What is a Frame in AI?
A frame is an object-oriented data structure used to divide knowledge into substructures by representing stereotyped situations, entities, or concepts.
8. What is meant by a slot in frame structures?
A slot is an attribute or property within a frame. For example, a "Car" frame might have a "Color" slot.
9. Define Propositional Logic.
Propositional Logic is a branch of logic that deals with propositions (statements that are objectively true or false) and their combinations using logical connectives.
10. What is a proposition?
A proposition is a declarative statement that has a definitive truth value—it must be either strictly True or False, but not both.
11. Name any two logical connectives.
1) AND (Conjunction, $\land$)
2) OR (Disjunction, $\lor$).
12. What is First Order Logic (FOL)?
FOL is an expressive logical system extending propositional logic by incorporating objects, predicates (relations), variables, and quantifiers.
13. What is a predicate?
A predicate represents a property of an object or a relationship between multiple objects, evaluating to true or false (e.g., Brother(John, Mary)).
14. Define quantifiers in FOL.
Quantifiers are logical symbols used to specify the scope of truth for a formula over a specific domain of variables.
15. What is universal quantifier ($\forall$)?
The universal quantifier implies that a statement holds True for all elements within a particular domain.
16. What is existential quantifier ($\exists$)?
The existential quantifier implies that there exists at least one element in the domain for which the statement holds True.
17. What is inference in AI?
Inference is the cognitive or computational process of deriving new knowledge or logical conclusions from existing facts and premises.
18. What is an inference rule?
An inference rule is a standard, logically sound template used to deduce a conclusion from a given set of premises (e.g., Modus Ponens).

B. 5 Mark Questions (Short Answer / Explanation)

1. Explain Knowledge Representation and its importance in AI.
Knowledge Representation (KR) is the field of AI focused on designing formal languages to encode information about the world so that an AI system can utilize it. Importance: It forms the backbone of an agent's intelligence. Without proper KR, an agent cannot perform reasoning, learning, or planning. Good KR makes implicit knowledge explicit, reduces computational complexity, and allows the Inference Engine to efficiently deduce solutions.
2. Describe the architecture of a Knowledge-Based Agent with a neat diagram.
A Knowledge-Based Agent operates through two main components: a Knowledge Base (KB) and an Inference Engine. It interacts with the environment by receiving Percepts (inputs) and emitting Actions (outputs).
3. Explain the working of a Knowledge-Based System.
A Knowledge-Based System works through a cycle of three primary operations:
  • TELL: The system is fed new knowledge or percepts from the environment, updating the Knowledge Base.
  • ASK: The agent queries the Knowledge Base to figure out what action it should take based on its current logic.
  • PERFORM: The agent executes the logically deduced action in the environment.
4. Discuss different types of knowledge with examples.
  • Declarative Knowledge: Knowing facts. Example: "The capital of France is Paris."
  • Procedural Knowledge: Knowing how to do something. Example: The sequence of steps to bake a cake.
  • Heuristic Knowledge: Rules of thumb derived from expert experience. Example: "If the computer won't turn on, check the power cable first."
  • Meta-knowledge: Knowledge about knowledge. Example: Knowing which algorithm to use to search a database efficiently.
6. Compare Propositional Logic and First Order Logic.
  • Propositional Logic: Deals with simple, indivisible sentences that are entirely true or false. It uses simple variables (P, Q) and lacks the expressiveness to talk about objects and their properties generally.
  • First Order Logic (FOL): Highly expressive. It breaks down propositions into Objects (John, Tree), Relations/Predicates (IsBrotherOf), and allows for Quantifiers ($\forall, \exists$) to make sweeping logical statements across domains.
7. Explain syntax and semantics of Propositional Logic.
Syntax: Defines the valid structure of sentences. It includes atomic propositions (A, B) and logical connectives like NOT ($\neg$), AND ($\land$), OR ($\lor$), IMPLIES ($\Rightarrow$), and IFF ($\Leftrightarrow$). Valid sentences must be formed using these symbols properly.

Semantics: Defines the meaning or truth value of sentences. Semantics are typically represented using truth tables, which compute the exact truth value of complex propositions based on the truth values of their atomic parts.
10. What is Inference in FOL? Explain any two inference rules.
Inference in FOL is the formal process of deriving new logical sentences from a given set of axioms in the Knowledge Base.
  • Modus Ponens: If $P \Rightarrow Q$ is true, and $P$ is true, then $Q$ is true.
  • Modus Tollens: If $P \Rightarrow Q$ is true, and $\neg Q$ (not Q) is true, then $\neg P$ must be true.
11. Explain forward chaining and backward chaining.
  • Forward Chaining: A data-driven approach. It starts with known facts and applies inference rules to extract new data until a desired goal or conclusion is reached.
  • Backward Chaining: A goal-driven approach. It starts with the target goal and works backward, looking for the facts and rules in the knowledge base that support or prove that goal.

C. 10 Mark Questions (Long Answer / Essay Type)

1. Explain Knowledge Representation in detail. Discuss its goals, issues, and methods.
Knowledge Representation (KR) is the process of defining the world in a structured format so that a computer can perform cognitive tasks like reasoning and problem-solving.

Goals of KR:
  • Expressiveness: The language should be rich enough to represent complex real-world concepts.
  • Inferential Adequacy: The system should be able to manipulate representations to derive new knowledge.
  • Acquisition Efficiency: It should be easy to inject new information into the system.
Issues in KR:
  • Representing exceptions to rules (e.g., "Birds fly, but penguins don't").
  • Handling ambiguity and uncertainty.
  • Balancing the trade-off between how expressive the logic is and how computationally complex it becomes to solve.
Methods: Common methods include Formal Logic (Propositional and First Order), Semantic Networks (graph-based relations), Frames (object-based attribute lists), and Production Rules (If-Then statements).
2. Describe Knowledge-Based Agents in detail with their structure and working.
A Knowledge-Based Agent is distinct because it relies on an explicit internal representation of knowledge rather than just hard-coded reactions.

Structure: It consists of two massive pillars: The Knowledge Base (KB), which is a set of sentences or rules expressed in a formal language, and the Inference Engine, which is the algorithm that manipulates those sentences to deduce new truths.

Working: When the agent perceives an environment, it translates that percept into a logical statement and adds it to the KB (using a TELL command). It then queries the KB (using an ASK command) to decide what action is logically optimal given the current state and its historical rules. Finally, it executes the action and updates its KB to remember what it did. This allows the agent to act rationally even in unseen, complex scenarios.
8. What is Reasoning in AI? Explain various types of reasoning with suitable examples.
Reasoning is the process an AI uses to draw conclusions, make predictions, or construct explanations from existing knowledge.

Types of Reasoning:
  • Deductive Reasoning: Guarantees the truth of the conclusion. (Rule: All cars have wheels. Fact: This is a car. Conclusion: It has wheels.)
  • Inductive Reasoning: Draws general conclusions from specific samples. (Observation: The first 5 taxis I saw were yellow. Conclusion: All taxis are yellow. *Not guaranteed to be true*).
  • Abductive Reasoning: Finding the most plausible explanation for an observation. (Fact: The ground is wet. Conclusion: It probably rained.)
  • Monotonic Reasoning: Once a fact is proven, it cannot be unproven by adding new facts. Traditional logic works this way.
  • Non-Monotonic Reasoning: Conclusions can be invalidated by new facts. (Fact: Birds fly. Fact: Tweety is a bird. Conclusion: Tweety flies. *New Fact*: Tweety is a penguin. *Updated Conclusion*: Tweety cannot fly.)
10. Discuss the importance of logic and reasoning in building intelligent agents and systems.
Logic and reasoning are the bedrock of what makes an agent "intelligent" rather than just a programmed machine.

1. Handling Complexity: Without logic, developers would have to hard-code an action for every conceivable scenario in the universe. Logic allows agents to store general rules and figure out the specific answers on the fly.
2. Proving Safety: In critical domains like autonomous driving or medical AI, formal logic allows engineers to mathematically verify that the system's reasoning processes will not yield dangerous conclusions.
3. Explainability: Reasoning engines can trace their steps backward. If an expert system diagnoses a patient with cancer, it can use its reasoning chain to explain exactly why it made that decision to the doctor.
4. Planning: Logic enables agents to look into the future, reasoning about the consequences of their actions before they take them, which is essential for problem-solving robots.

Software Testing - Question Bank

1. What is Software Testing? (Also answers Q6)
Software testing is the process of evaluating and verifying that a software product or application does what it is supposed to do. The benefits of testing include preventing bugs, reducing development costs, and improving performance.
2. Define Software Quality Assurance (SQA).
Software Quality Assurance (SQA) is a proactive, process-oriented practice that ensures the software development processes, methodologies, and standards are correctly defined and followed to guarantee the final product meets specified quality standards.
3. When is Decision table testing used?
Decision table testing is used when a system's behavior depends on a complex set of logical combinations (multiple conditions). It is a black-box testing technique used to capture complex business rules and ensure all possible combinations of inputs are tested.
4. What are the benefits of designing tests early in the life cycle? (Also answers Q13)
  • Cost Reduction: Finding and fixing defects during the requirement or design phase is significantly cheaper than fixing them after development.
  • Clarity: It helps identify ambiguities, contradictions, or missing details in the requirements early on.
  • Time Saving: Test execution can begin immediately once the code is ready.
5. Define Software faults.
A software fault (commonly known as a bug or defect) is a flaw, mistake, or incorrect step in a computer program that causes it to produce an incorrect or unanticipated result or to behave in unintended ways.
7. Define RTM.
RTM stands for Requirements Traceability Matrix. It is a document that maps and traces user requirements with corresponding test cases to ensure that all requirements defined for a system are tested and verified (achieving 100% test coverage).
8. What is Unit Testing?
Unit testing is the first level of software testing where individual units or components of a software are tested in isolation. The purpose is to validate that each unit of the software performs exactly as designed. It is usually performed by the developers.
9. What is Boundary Value Analysis?
Boundary Value Analysis (BVA) is a black-box testing technique based on testing the extreme ends or boundaries between partitions of input values. Errors are most frequently found at the boundaries rather than the center of an input domain. Test values include the minimum, minimum-1, maximum, maximum+1, and typical values.
10. Define Test Case.
A test case is a specific set of conditions, variables, or inputs under which a tester evaluates whether a system or a specific software feature works correctly and satisfies its requirements. It includes preconditions, inputs, expected results, and post-conditions.
11. Discuss the Waterfall Model.
The Waterfall Model is a traditional, linear-sequential software development life cycle (SDLC) model. Each phase must be completely finished before the next phase begins, and there is no overlapping.

The sequence is: Requirements → Design → Implementation (Coding) → Testing → Deployment → Maintenance. It is simple but rigid, making it difficult to adapt to changes once a phase is complete.
12. List the different Methodologies in Agile Development Model. (Also answers Q29)
  • Scrum: Focuses on short iterations called sprints and daily stand-up meetings.
  • Kanban: Focuses on continuous delivery and visual management of tasks using a Kanban board.
  • Extreme Programming (XP): Focuses on technical excellence, pair programming, and frequent releases.
  • Feature-Driven Development (FDD): Focuses on building models and building by feature.
  • Lean Software Development: Focuses on eliminating waste and amplifying learning.
14. Explain AI-driven test case generation.
AI-driven test case generation uses machine learning algorithms and natural language processing to automatically read software requirements, analyze source code, or monitor user behavior to generate optimal and high-coverage test cases without human intervention. This speeds up the testing phase and identifies edge cases that manual testers might miss.
15. Develop a program to implement a letter grading procedure. Determine basis paths and derive cases. (Also answers Q31)
Assumed Logic: If marks $\ge 90$ (Grade A), else if marks $\ge 80$ (Grade B), else (Grade C).

Pseudocode: 
1. Input marks
2. If (marks >= 90)
3.    Return "A"
4. Else If (marks >= 80)
5.    Return "B"
6. Else
7.    Return "C"
8. End
Basis Paths (Independent Execution Paths):
  • Path 1: 1 → 2 → 3 → 8 (Evaluates True for $\ge 90$)
  • Path 2: 1 → 2 → 4 → 5 → 8 (Evaluates False for $\ge 90$, True for $\ge 80$)
  • Path 3: 1 → 2 → 4 → 6 → 7 → 8 (Evaluates False for both, default)
Derived Test Cases:
  • Test Case 1 (covers Path 1): Input = 95. Expected Result = A
  • Test Case 2 (covers Path 2): Input = 85. Expected Result = B
  • Test Case 3 (covers Path 3): Input = 75. Expected Result = C
16. Construct the SDLC with the help of a neat diagram. (Also answers Q27 & Q34)
The Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is the structured process used for developing software.
18. Explain the procedure of Manual testing? Mention the advantages.
Procedure: 1) Analyze requirements. 2) Write test plan and test cases. 3) Set up the testing environment. 4) Execute test cases manually step-by-step as an end-user without automation tools. 5) Log defects for any failed tests. 6) Re-test once fixed.

Advantages: Enables exploratory testing, allows evaluation of UX/UI visually, no programming knowledge required, cheaper for short-term projects, and catches easily noticeable human errors.
19. A wholesaler sells printer cartridges (Min order 5, Discount for 100+). Prepare test cases using Boundary Value Analysis.
Partitions:
  • Invalid (Too low): $ < 5 $
  • Valid (No Discount): $ 5 $ to $ 99 $
  • Valid (Discount): $ \ge 100 $
Boundary Values to Test: 4, 5, 6 (Lower Boundary) and 99, 100, 101 (Upper Boundary).

Groups of 3 test inputs generated using BVA:
Group 1 (Testing Minimum Boundary): { 4 (Invalid), 5 (Valid-Min), 6 (Valid) }
Group 2 (Testing Discount Boundary): { 99 (Valid-No Discount), 100 (Valid-Discount-Min), 101 (Valid-Discount) }
21. Discuss the V-Model with the help of its Demerits and Merits.
The V-Model (Verification and Validation model) is an extension of the Waterfall model where testing phases are planned in parallel with a corresponding phase of development, forming a V-shape.
Merits: Highly disciplined; testing activities start early; easy to understand and manage; defects are found early reducing cost.
Demerits: Extremely rigid; very difficult to go back and change functionality; not suited for dynamic or agile projects.

A. Short Answer Questions

1. What is cloud computing?
Cloud computing is the delivery of computing services—including servers, storage, databases, networking, software, and analytics—over the internet ("the cloud") to offer faster innovation, flexible resources, and economies of scale.
2. Define Cloud.
A cloud is a global network of remote servers hosted on the internet that are used to store, manage, and process data, rather than using a local server or a personal computer.
3. Write the differences between IaaS and PaaS.
  • IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service): Provides raw computing resources like virtual machines, networking, and storage. You have to manage the OS and runtime yourself.
  • PaaS (Platform as a Service): Provides a complete development environment (OS, runtime, databases) built on top of IaaS. Developers use it to build and deploy apps without worrying about managing the underlying servers.
4. Mention the differences between SaaS and IaaS.
  • SaaS (Software as a Service): Delivers fully functional software applications directly to end-users over the internet (e.g., Gmail, Google Docs). No IT management required.
  • IaaS: Delivers virtualized hardware resources to IT administrators or developers who use it to build their own systems and software.
5. State the purpose of Hypervisor.
A hypervisor (or Virtual Machine Monitor) is software that creates, runs, and manages virtual machines (VMs). Its main purpose is to isolate the VMs from one another and dynamically allocate the physical hardware resources (CPU, RAM) to them safely and efficiently.
6. What is Virtualization?
Virtualization is the process of creating a software-based (virtual) representation of something rather than a physical one. It can apply to applications, servers, storage, and networks.
7. Mention three major components of Virtualized Environments.
1) The Hardware (Host Machine), 2) The Hypervisor / VMM (Virtual Machine Monitor), and 3) The Virtual Machine (Guest OS and Applications).
8. What is Virtualization Layer?
The virtualization layer is a software layer (typically the hypervisor) that sits between the physical hardware and the operating systems. It abstracts the physical hardware resources so they can be partitioned and shared among multiple virtual machines.
9. List out the Characteristics of Virtualized Environments.
The key characteristics are: 1) Increased Security/Isolation, 2) Encapsulation of VMs into files, 3) Hardware Independence, 4) Multiplexing and Resource Sharing, and 5) Portability/Migration.
10. What do you mean by deployment models / software?
Cloud deployment models indicate where the infrastructure resides and who manages it. The four primary deployment models are Public Cloud, Private Cloud, Hybrid Cloud, and Community Cloud.

B. Detailed / Essay Type Questions

1. Briefly describe the architecture of cloud computing with diagram.
Cloud computing architecture is divided into two main sections connected via the internet:
  • Front-End (Client side): The interface and applications that the user interacts with (e.g., a web browser or mobile app).
  • Back-End (Cloud provider side): The massive pool of resources including servers, storage systems, databases, security mechanisms, and management models.
2. Discuss the public, private, community, and hybrid deployment models.
  • Public Cloud: Services are owned and operated by a third-party provider and delivered over the public internet (e.g., AWS, Google Cloud). Highly scalable and cost-effective, but offers less control.
  • Private Cloud: Cloud infrastructure used exclusively by a single business or organization. It can be physically located at the company's on-site datacenter. Offers maximum control and security, but is expensive.
  • Community Cloud: Infrastructure shared by several organizations that have shared concerns (e.g., compliance, security). It is managed by the organizations or a third party.
  • Hybrid Cloud: Combines public and private clouds, bound together by technology that allows data and applications to be shared between them. Gives businesses greater flexibility and more deployment options.
3. Demonstrate the Services offered by Cloud providers in detail with a diagram.
Cloud services are typically divided into a stack of three broad categories:
  • IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service): At the base, it provides the fundamental hardware—servers, virtual machines, storage, and networks.
  • PaaS (Platform as a Service): In the middle, it provides an environment for developers to build, test, and deploy software applications without managing the underlying servers.
  • SaaS (Software as a Service): At the top, it provides a complete, ready-to-use software application delivered over the internet (e.g., Microsoft 365, Salesforce).
4. Illustrate any five benefits and Limitations of cloud computing.
Benefits:
  1. Cost Efficiency: Eliminates capital expense of buying hardware and data centers (Pay-as-you-go).
  2. Scalability: Resources can be scaled up or down instantly based on demand.
  3. Accessibility: Data and applications can be accessed from anywhere with an internet connection.
  4. Disaster Recovery: Cloud data backup makes recovery from hardware failures much easier.
  5. Automatic Updates: Software and security patches are updated by the provider automatically.
Limitations:
  1. Internet Dependency: You cannot access your data or applications without a stable network.
  2. Security and Privacy: Entrusting sensitive data to third-party providers raises security risks.
  3. Vendor Lock-in: It can be difficult and expensive to migrate from one cloud provider to another.
  4. Limited Control: The customer has limited control and access to the backend infrastructure.
  5. Downtime: Even the biggest cloud providers experience occasional outages.
5. Examine and Explain the needs of hypervisor and their types with diagram.
Needs of a Hypervisor: A hypervisor is essential because it safely isolates different operating systems running on the same hardware. It handles the dynamic allocation of CPU, memory, and storage to each virtual machine, ensuring that a crash in one VM does not affect the others.

Types of Hypervisors:
  • Type 1 (Bare-Metal): Installed directly on the physical hardware. Highly efficient and secure. Used in enterprise data centers (e.g., VMware ESXi).
  • Type 2 (Hosted): Installed on top of an existing host operating system (like Windows or Mac). Slower, but great for personal testing and development (e.g., VirtualBox, VMware Workstation).
6. Elaborate the five Levels of Virtualization and their functionalities (Machine Reference Model).
The Taxonomy of Virtualization Techniques divides virtualization into 5 functional levels:
  1. Instruction Set Architecture (ISA) Level: Virtualization is performed by emulating a given ISA. It allows executing legacy code compiled for one processor on a different processor.
  2. Hardware Abstraction Level (HAL): Virtualization is performed right above the bare hardware. This is where hypervisors (VMMs) operate, generating virtual hardware environments for VMs.
  3. Operating System Level: Creates isolated containers or "jails" on a single physical server and OS instance. All virtualized environments share the same underlying OS kernel (e.g., Docker).
  4. Library Support Level: Virtualization by intercepting library calls. It creates execution environments for applications by emulating APIs (e.g., WINE allowing Windows apps to run on Linux).
  5. User-Application Level: Virtualizes an application so it behaves like a standalone runtime environment. (e.g., Java Virtual Machine - JVM).
7. Demonstrate how to perform hardware virtualization with examples.
Hardware virtualization involves abstracting physical hardware into virtual instances using a Hypervisor. There are three main approaches:
  • Full Virtualization: The hypervisor completely simulates hardware. The Guest OS is entirely unaware it is virtualized and does not need modification. (Example: VMware ESXi).
  • Para-virtualization: The Guest OS is modified to know it is running in a virtual environment. Instead of simulating hardware, the Guest OS communicates directly with the hypervisor, offering better performance. (Example: Xen).
  • Hardware-Assisted Virtualization: Physical CPUs have built-in instructions to assist virtualization, taking the heavy processing load off the hypervisor software. (Example: Intel VT-x and AMD-V).
8. Analyze the concepts of Network virtualization and Storage virtualization.
  • Network Virtualization: Abstracting physical network resources (routers, switches) into a single software-based administrative entity. It allows multiple isolated virtual networks to run over the same physical network. Example: Software-Defined Networking (SDN) and VLANs.
  • Storage Virtualization: Pooling physical storage from multiple network storage devices into what appears to be a single, centrally managed storage device. It helps in easy backup, archiving, and recovery. Example: Storage Area Networks (SAN).
9. Illustrate the types of Server virtualization, Desktop virtualization and Data virtualization.
  • Server Virtualization: Masking physical server resources from users. It involves dividing a single physical server into multiple isolated virtual servers. This increases hardware utilization and saves space/power.
  • Desktop Virtualization: Also known as VDI (Virtual Desktop Infrastructure). It hosts a desktop operating system (like Windows 10) within a virtual machine running on a centralized server. Users can access their "desktop" from any thin client or device remotely.
  • Data Virtualization: An approach to data management that allows an application to retrieve and manipulate data without requiring technical details about the data, such as how it is formatted at the source or where it is physically located.
